Problem

Customer care agents who use Sprinklr's Modern Care software often need assistance from fellow agents to address customer queries and incidents that come are assigned to them. This assistance must be provided quickly as cases are time sensitive. There was no integrated feature that enabled such collaboration between agents where they could reach out for help or answer others' doubts.

Solution

I designed a feature to be integrated into Modern Care that would allow agents to reach out to other members of their team and get the information they need quickly to solve cases. This feature would lead to a smoother way of collaborating for agents than what they were doing previously.

Research - Interviews

The scope of the project started out as very broad and it was up to me to carry out the process of problem identification to narrow my scope. I did in-depth structured interviews with 3 customer care agents who used Modern Care. Notes from all three interviews revealed that there was often a need to ask for help when tackling cases and this need was being met through fairly makeshift ways.

Key Insights

  • 15% to 20% of all cases require multiple people from the team to be involved.
  • Majority of Care Agents possess less than a year of experience and often require assistance from senior agents to resolve cases.
  • Asking for assistance is confined to either emails which take too long when dealing with time sensitive cases, or to third party social channels like Slack with no integration.
  • Current collaboration is slow and disorganised.

Existing Solutions

Slack is a popular collaboration platform. The customer care agents that I interviewed said that they used Slack as a tool for asking for assistance from other agents. While this worked, it was a makeshift solution. Agents had to keep jumping back and forth between the Agent Console and Slack to interact with other agents. A more seamless solution was required.

Solution Requirements

There were a few factors which needed to be considered while deciding what kind of collaboration model was needed.

üë®üèª‚Äçüíª Requirement 1 - Fit Workflows

There were largely two categories of agents - senior and junior. Junior agents often had less than 6 months of experience in their roles and would frequently ask doubts and queries to senior agents to resolve cases. These were the agents who would handle the majority of the case load. The senior agents had more experience and dealt mostly with high priority cases only or cases that had been escalated to them through junior agents. A large part of their job was assisting junior agents in solving cases. While both types of agents worked on the agent console, their work profiles and processes were different. Thus any solution to the problem would have to fit into both types of workflows.

üìÅ Requirement 2 - Maintain Records

Keeping a record of all the interactions that any agent has had with a customer is important since this history of incidents helps agents to better understand the context which the customer is coming from and hence more effectively satisfy their needs. Any assistance feature would also, by extension, need to have records of all the communications between agents that have happened concerning that customer.

‚ö°Ô∏è Requirement 3 - Quick

Since the cases being dealt with are on channels like Whatsapp, Twitter Messages etc. customers expect quick replies. This means that any process of asking for assistance must also be quick.

Iteration #1 -

This was a relatively straightforward concept with a separate pane that would slide out from the left over the Agent Console interface. It would have an option to create new queries and send it to the people who an agent would want assistance from. Similarly, queries started by other agents would show up in the same queue if they had addressed the query to that agent.

Limitations of this Concept

1. The issue with this concept was that the separate pane for all queries defeated the point of efficiency. It forced agents to split their attention between two spaces (the agent console and the query board) and hence compromised the workflow.

2. Furthermore, these queries did not appear with the cases they were about and hence were not useful as records of previous conversations.

Final Design - Discussions Concept

The final design I came up with which solved the above issues had a different approach altogether. I took the assistance feature out of the separate pane and situated it within the right side along with the rest of the case related information. An agent would thus be able to start a discussion in this space which was contextually tied to the case. This would make sure any and all discussions happening on the case stays linked to it for future reference.

This left the problem of incoming queries which I solved by introducing a floating window on to the agent console interface which could be minimised. This arrangement would cause the least amount of distraction for an agent busy with his/her own cases. Incoming queries would show up in the floating window which the agent can answer when possible.

Learnings and Reflections

  • When designing for users who face huge amounts of workload on a certain platform, any feature that is added has to interfere as less as possible with the main workflow or else its drawbacks will outweigh its benefits.

  • Designing collaborative platforms is tricky since it involves defining social relationships on a digital platform. I consciously decided to keep the discussions open and flexible by giving the option to add anyone or any group of people to the conversation instead of defining rigid roles. I did this because in the real world teams of agents don't operate based on roles when helping each other out, they do so in an informal manner.

  • Every UI decision was a nightmare to make since even the tiniest differences would not fit either the function or the semantics of the feature. A chat had to look like a chat and a link had to look like a link to capitalise on the existing knowledge of agents and make the interface easy to use.
